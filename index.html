The Indelible Mark of Democracy

The Story of India's Election Ink

Introduction

India, the world's largest democracy, uses a unique tool to ensure the integrity of its electoral process: indelible ink. This ink marks voters' fingers to prevent multiple voting, symbolizing the transparency and fairness of Indian elections.

History

The idea of using indelible ink was proposed by India's first Chief Election Commissioner, Sukumar Sen. Developed by the National Physical Laboratory (NPL) in the 1950s, the ink has been a staple in Indian elections since the third general elections in 1962.

Composition

The key component is silver nitrate which stain the skin in 40 seconds upon exposure to ultraviolet light. It contains solvent such as alcohol for faster drying. This stain is impossible to wash off and remains visible until external skin cells are replaced.

Manufacturing

The manufacturing of this special ink is handled by Mysore Paints and Varnish Limited (MPVL) in Karnataka. Established in 1937, MPVL is the sole supplier of indelible ink for Indian elections and exports it to over 35 countries.

Evolution

Initially, black ink was used, but it was later changed to purple to ensure visibility on all skin tones. This change was crucial in maintaining the ink's effectiveness and visibility.

Applications

Besides elections, indelible ink has been used in polio immunization drives and during the demonetization period to mark those who exchanged currency notes.
